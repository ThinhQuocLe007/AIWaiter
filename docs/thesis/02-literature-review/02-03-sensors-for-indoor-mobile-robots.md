# 2.3 Sensors for Indoor Mobile Robots

To move around on its own, an indoor mobile robot has to answer two questions at every moment: what is around it, and how is it moving. No single sensor answers both, so the robot carries several complementary sensors - some looking outward at the environment, others measuring the robot's own motion.

A 2D LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) is the robot's main sensor for perceiving the environment. It rotates a laser rangefinder through a full circle and, for each direction, sends out a short laser pulse and measures the time it takes to reflect back from the nearest surface. Because the speed of light is known, each time-of-flight is converted into a distance, so one full rotation produces a set of distance readings all around the robot - a 360-degree "slice" of the space at the height of the sensor. This slice, a set of points describing where the nearest obstacles are, is what the robot uses to build a map of a room and to detect walls, furniture, and other obstacles while it drives. A 2D LiDAR is accurate and works in any lighting, including the dark, but it only sees one horizontal plane, so it can miss objects that are above or below that plane.

An RGB-D camera adds information that a 2D LiDAR cannot provide. It captures an ordinary color image (the RGB part) and, at the same time, a depth image (the D part) in which every pixel carries the distance from the camera to the surface seen in that pixel. Most indoor RGB-D cameras obtain depth by projecting an invisible infrared pattern onto the scene and viewing it with two infrared sensors, comparing the two views to work out how far away each point is, similar to how two eyes give a sense of depth. Because it sees in three dimensions and in color, the camera can detect obstacles at any height - including low objects and the edges of tables that a 2D LiDAR would miss - and it can recognize visual patterns in the scene, which later allows the robot to find the fiducial markers placed at each table. Its main limitations are a narrower field of view than the LiDAR and a sensitivity to strong lighting and reflective surfaces.

An IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit) measures the robot's own motion rather than the environment. The type used here is a six-axis IMU, which combines a three-axis gyroscope and a three-axis accelerometer in one chip: the gyroscope measures how fast the robot is rotating about each axis, and the accelerometer measures the accelerations acting on it, including the constant pull of gravity. From these six signals the robot can estimate how it is turning and, in particular, which way it is facing. The IMU updates very quickly and does not depend on the wheels touching the ground, so it captures fast rotations well; however, because its heading is obtained by adding up many small rotation measurements over time, tiny errors accumulate and the estimated heading slowly drifts.

A wheel encoder is attached to each driven wheel to measure how much that wheel has turned. It is an incremental encoder: as the wheel rotates, the encoder produces a stream of electrical pulses, and counting these pulses tells how far the wheel has rotated, while the pattern of two slightly offset pulse channels tells the direction of rotation. Knowing how much each wheel has turned, together with the size of the wheels and the distance between them, lets the robot estimate how far and in which direction it has moved - this estimate of motion from wheel rotation is called odometry. Wheel encoders are simple, cheap, and give a smooth, high-rate estimate of motion, but they assume the wheels roll without slipping; whenever a wheel slips or skids, the estimate gains a small error that, like the IMU, accumulates over time. Combining the encoders with the IMU, so that the strengths of one cover the weaknesses of the other, is the subject of the localization and fusion methods in the later sections.
