## 2.3 Vietnamese Voice Understanding

> *A customer speaks Vietnamese into a microphone mounted on the robot; a spoken Vietnamese reply comes back through its speaker. Three components stand between those two events — voice activity detection, speech-to-text, and speech synthesis — and each is available as a mature off-the-shelf model. This section surveys the options for each and what distinguishes them, and characterises the setting they would have to operate in — a shared edge device, a noisy room, and a conversation with a rhythm — noting throughout what the published evaluations leave unestablished about behaviour under those conditions. The edge hardware itself is surveyed in §2.8.*

A restaurant voice interaction follows a physical path: the customer speaks into a microphone mounted on the robot, the captured audio is processed on the robot's edge computer, and a spoken reply is produced through the robot's speaker. That path must complete quickly enough to sustain conversational rhythm. It must operate under restaurant acoustic conditions — ambient noise in the region of 60–70 dB from concurrent conversations, kitchen sounds, plate clatter, and chair movement [2.3.22]. It must process Vietnamese, a tonal language in which a single diacritic changes word meaning entirely. And it must do all of this on hardware that is simultaneously running the robot's navigation stack.

Three properties of that setting bear on every component surveyed below, and none of them is a property of the components themselves. The first concerns **connectivity**: a component that requires a network round-trip per utterance behaves differently in a building with intermittent WiFi than one that does not, since a temporary outage becomes a total loss of the voice interface rather than a degradation of it. The second concerns **memory**: the edge device provides a single pool shared between the voice pipeline, the navigation stack, and the operating system, so every model's footprint is subtracted from a fixed total rather than drawn from a dedicated allocation (§2.8). The third concerns **latency**, and it is the least obvious of the three because it is not a property of any single component.

The latency budget is a sum, and the components compete for it. An utterance is not complete until the detector has observed enough trailing silence to declare it finished; that silence window is dead time in every turn, and it is paid before transcription begins. Transcription then scales with model size, synthesis scales with sentence length, and the language model sits between them. Because the total is what the customer experiences, the correct question for each component is not "which option is most accurate" but "how much of the budget does this option consume, and what does the accuracy gained cost the components downstream." The remainder of this section presents the available options for each component and the properties that determine that trade-off.

### 2.3.1 Voice Activity Detection

Voice activity detection determines the boundaries of a spoken utterance in a continuous audio stream — when the customer started speaking, and when they stopped. It is the first processing stage, and its output, a trimmed segment containing exactly one utterance, feeds directly into the transcription model. If detection ends an utterance prematurely, the transcriber receives a truncated sentence and the agent never sees the complete order. If detection triggers on background noise, the entire downstream pipeline — transcription, intent classification, reasoning, validation — processes restaurant clatter as though it were an order. The accuracy of this single stage therefore bounds everything that follows.

The simplest approach classifies any audio frame whose root-mean-square amplitude exceeds a fixed threshold as speech. This works in a quiet recording environment, where silence sits near zero amplitude and speech rises clearly above it. In a restaurant it does not: the ambient noise floor regularly exceeds the amplitude of quiet speech, and no threshold separates the two. Raising it causes trailing syllables to be lost; lowering it produces continuous false triggers. Energy thresholding has no mechanism to distinguish speech from non-speech at comparable loudness, which makes it unsuitable here regardless of tuning [2.3.1].

Lightweight neural models address this by classifying frames on learned spectral structure rather than amplitude. Silero VAD is a compact model of roughly 2 MB that processes frames on CPU in real time, emits a speech probability per frame, and exposes a configurable decision threshold [2.3.2]. WebRTC VAD, roughly 100 KB, applies a Gaussian mixture model trained on telephony speech; it is the lighter of the two and correspondingly less accurate under noise [2.3.3]. Both run without GPU involvement, which matters because GPU memory on the edge device is committed to transcription and to the navigation stack.

At the accurate end of the range, systems such as pyannote.audio [2.3.4] and NVIDIA NeMo's VAD [2.3.5] use substantially larger architectures to achieve state-of-the-art frame-level discrimination. Both expect GPU inference for real-time operation. On a device where the transcription model and the navigation stack already contend for a shared memory pool, committing further GPU capacity to an always-on detector is difficult to justify when CPU-only alternatives are adequate.

Two parameters govern the behaviour of any detector in this class, and they are where the accuracy and latency constraints meet. The first is the decision threshold on the per-frame speech probability, which trades two failure modes against one another: set high, it suppresses false triggers from impulse noise at the cost of clipping quiet onsets; set low, it captures hesitant speech at the cost of admitting noise to the transcriber. The second follows from the fact that any system segmenting continuous audio into discrete utterances must decide when an utterance has ended, and the conventional criterion is a fixed interval of observed silence. That interval is a floor on turn latency independent of every other component in the pipeline: it elapses on every turn, before transcription begins, and no downstream optimization recovers it. Shortening it returns control to the speaker sooner and truncates anyone who pauses mid-sentence. Neither parameter has a value that is correct in the abstract — both are properties of the room and of the people speaking in it.

The published record supplies neither those values nor the evidence from which they could be inferred. Silero VAD has been evaluated on multilingual telephone speech and meeting recordings in quiet to moderately noisy conditions; WebRTC VAD on telephony-quality speech. Neither has been characterized on Vietnamese speech, nor under a restaurant noise profile combining sustained broadband kitchen noise, impulse events from dropped utensils, and intelligible conversation at adjacent tables — the last being the hardest case, because competing speech is precisely what a speech detector is built to respond to. What the literature does establish is narrower, and concerns the approaches rather than their settings: which of them run without GPU involvement, what each costs in memory, and whether a decision threshold is exposed for tuning at all. The values those parameters should take in a room full of diners are not derivable from any of it.

### 2.3.2 Speech-to-Text for Vietnamese

Speech-to-text converts the segment isolated by the detector into Vietnamese text. It is the most consequential stage in the pipeline: every component downstream — the intent classifier, the agent's language model, the validator, the response generator — operates on the text this stage produces, and none can recover information that transcription destroyed. A tone error that turns *cá* (fish) into *cà* (eggplant) does not present as an error downstream; it presents as a correctly-processed order for the wrong dish.

The dominant architecture for on-device multilingual transcription is Whisper, a Transformer encoder-decoder trained on approximately 680,000 hours of multilingual web audio [2.3.6]. Audio passes through a convolutional front end, is encoded into a latent representation, and is decoded autoregressively into text conditioned on both the audio encoding and the tokens generated so far. Vietnamese is present in the training distribution but was not a primary target, so the model handles it competently without being optimized for it. The family scales across four sizes — tiny (39M parameters), base (74M), medium (769M), and large-v3 (1.55B) — trading accuracy against memory and inference time.

The deployment characteristics of this family were changed substantially by faster-whisper, a reimplementation built on the CTranslate2 inference engine [2.3.7]. CTranslate2 applies operator fusion, memory-layout optimization, and integer quantization to reduce both latency and memory footprint relative to the reference implementation. Two consequences matter for edge deployment. First, a model size that would otherwise exceed the available budget becomes viable. Second — and less often noted — the models are distributed *already converted* to the CTranslate2 format, so deploying one requires no weight-conversion step: the runtime fetches and loads it directly.

That second property is what separates the multilingual models from the Vietnamese-specialized alternative. PhoWhisper [2.3.8] fine-tunes Whisper on Vietnamese speech data and reports improved word error rate over the multilingual base on Vietnamese benchmarks, with the gains concentrated in tonal diacritics — exactly the dimension on which a general multilingual model is weakest for this language, and exactly the dimension on which a restaurant ordering system is most exposed. It is distributed, however, as Transformers-format checkpoints, and running it under CTranslate2 requires an explicit conversion of the weights before deployment. What that conversion does *not* cost is memory. Because a fine-tune preserves the architecture and parameter count of its base, PhoWhisper at a given size occupies exactly what the multilingual model occupies at the same size once both are in CTranslate2 format — the Vietnamese advantage is free in footprint and free at inference time, and is purchased only with a build step and a locally maintained model artefact, against a model the runtime retrieves and loads unattended.

Cloud services occupy a different position entirely. Google Cloud Speech-to-Text, Viettel AI, and FPT.AI all provide dedicated Vietnamese recognition trained on large Vietnamese corpora and running on server-grade infrastructure [2.3.10]–[2.3.12], and their accuracy on clean Vietnamese speech exceeds what any edge-deployable model achieves. Their limitation is structural rather than acoustic: every utterance requires a network round-trip, which places conversational latency partly outside the system's control and makes a WiFi outage a total failure of the voice interface.

Word error rates are deliberately omitted from the table. Published figures for these systems are drawn from different corpora under different recording conditions and are not comparable cell to cell; the Whisper family's rates are reported against multilingual benchmarks [2.3.6] and PhoWhisper's against Vietnamese academic corpora including VLSP [2.3.8]–[2.3.9], which consist of read speech recorded in quiet conditions with standard pronunciation. A single ranked accuracy column would imply a comparability that the underlying evaluations do not support.

What the published record does characterise is what the table reports: parameter count, language targeting, distribution format, memory footprint, and whether operation requires a network. Of these, the distribution format is the least often noted and the most consequential in practice, because it determines whether obtaining a set of weights is a matter of loading them or of maintaining a converted artefact — and it is the one dimension on which the Vietnamese-specialized model and its multilingual base diverge, given that they are otherwise identical in architecture, footprint, and inference cost.

What the record does not characterise, for any entry in the table, is behaviour under the condition this application presents: conversational Vietnamese, spoken over sustained restaurant noise, containing compound dish names and informal register. The accuracy ordering among these options in that setting — including whether a model fine-tuned on read Vietnamese speech retains its advantage over a broadly-trained multilingual base once the audio leaves the recording studio — is not established by anything in the literature surveyed here.

### 2.3.3 Text-to-Speech for Vietnamese

The final stage converts the agent's Vietnamese text into audible speech. Quality here is judged on two axes that do not always move together: intelligibility, meaning the customer recovers the words, and naturalness, meaning the voice is appropriate to a service setting. Vietnamese adds a third consideration, since tone is lexical — a synthesizer that renders diacritics inaccurately does not merely sound unnatural, it says something else.

The available engines span a wide range of model complexity. At the lightest extreme, eSpeak-NG is a formant synthesizer: rather than learning from recorded speech, it models the vocal tract as a set of resonant frequencies and applies rules to shape them into phonemes [2.3.13]. The result is unmistakably mechanical — flat, without natural prosody — but it is approximately 5 MB, runs on any CPU, and its Vietnamese phoneme tables cover the full tonal system. Formant synthesis has served screen readers and accessibility tooling for decades and remains the floor of the range.

Piper occupies the middle [2.3.14]. It implements the VITS architecture, in which a single network converts text directly to a waveform in one pass, with no intermediate spectrogram and no separate vocoder [2.3.15]. The community-trained Vietnamese voice is roughly 200 MB and synthesizes a sentence on CPU in the region of half a second. Its output is audibly synthetic but fully intelligible, with tones rendered correctly. It is the only neural Vietnamese voice that operates within an edge memory budget without requiring GPU capacity.

At the upper end of on-device synthesis, Coqui's XTTS v2 uses a large autoregressive model with a separate vocoder, supports Vietnamese within its multilingual training, and offers voice cloning from a short reference clip [2.3.16]. Its naturalness approaches that of cloud neural voices. Its cost is roughly 4 GB of GPU memory — on a device already allocating memory to transcription and to navigation, this is the single largest claim any voice component could make, and it competes directly with the transcription model selected in §2.3.2.

The remaining options are hosted services. Microsoft Azure Neural TTS, reachable through the open-source edge-tts client, provides multiple Vietnamese voices covering Northern and Southern accents and both speaker genders [2.3.17]. Google Cloud TTS offers WaveNet voices with the highest reported naturalness [2.3.18]. Two Vietnamese providers, vbee and FPT.AI, supply voices trained specifically for the local market [2.3.19]–[2.3.20]. All four are more natural than any on-device option, and all four require connectivity for every sentence spoken.

Synthesis quality is conventionally assessed by Mean Opinion Score, in which listeners rate samples on a five-point naturalness scale under controlled conditions [2.3.21]. Reported scores consistently place cloud neural voices above on-device neural synthesis, and on-device neural synthesis well above formant methods. Specific values are not reproduced here: the published scores for these engines come from separate studies using different listener pools, different text material, and in most cases languages other than Vietnamese, so a single ranked column would imply a comparability that the underlying evaluations do not support. No MOS study covers Vietnamese restaurant-domain utterances, and none covers listening conditions with restaurant ambient noise present — a condition under which the gap between a moderately natural voice and a highly natural one may compress considerably, since intelligibility rather than naturalness becomes the limiting factor.

These engines span three orders of magnitude in footprint, and they divide on two properties the record does document unambiguously: whether synthesis requires a network, and whether it requires GPU capacity. Both divisions fall in the same place — the two on-device neural options sit on opposite sides of the GPU line, and every hosted service sits on the far side of the network line — so the footprint range is not a continuum of small trade-offs but a set of discrete commitments about where synthesis runs and what it displaces.

---

The properties on which these components differ — footprint, compute requirement, distribution format, dependence on a network — are documented, and the survey in §2.3.1 through §2.3.3 rests on that documentation. What the published record does not contain is any measurement taken under the acoustic and linguistic conditions in which such a pipeline would operate in a restaurant.

The evaluations cited in this section were conducted on read speech, telephony, or recorded meetings, in quiet or acoustically controlled conditions. Detection performance was characterised on telephone and meeting audio; transcription rates were measured on prepared corpora with standard pronunciation — including the Vietnamese corpora on which the Vietnamese-specific model reports its gains; naturalness was rated by listeners in quiet rooms. A dining room during service presents none of these conditions. It presents sustained broadband noise, impulse events from tableware, and intelligible speech from adjacent tables, the last being the hardest case for a speech detector, since competing speech is precisely what such a detector is built to respond to. The Vietnamese spoken in it is conversational, carrying compound dish names and informal register rather than prepared sentences. And the listening environment is one in which intelligibility rather than naturalness may be the property that determines whether a synthesised reply is understood at all.

The consequence is that the parameters governing such a pipeline's behaviour in deployment — the frame-level decision threshold, the silence interval that terminates an utterance, and the transcription accuracy attainable on restaurant-domain vocabulary — cannot be read from the literature. They are empirical quantities, and they are measured in §5.4.

A second observation concerns the shape of the interaction rather than the quality of any component. Voice assistants in the surveyed literature are single-device systems: one microphone, one speaker, one user, activated by a wake word and listening continuously thereafter. The arrangement required here is different in kind. Capture is armed deliberately, by a customer pressing a control on a tablet that is not the device holding the microphone; the command is routed through a server to whichever robot is currently serving that table, a binding that changes as robots move between tables; and the resulting turn must remain interruptible throughout, by a customer who cancels it, mutes the reply, or simply begins speaking over it. None of this is a property of the detection, transcription, or synthesis models, all of which are indifferent to how they are invoked. It is a property of the orchestration around them, and it has no counterpart in single-device voice assistants, whose activation model assumes that the microphone, the speaker, the control surface, and the user occupy the same place.
